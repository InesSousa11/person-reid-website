Abstract

Social robots operating in real-world environments must do more than detect and follow people moment by moment. To interact robustly over time, they must preserve identity despite occlusions, re-entry into the scene, changes in pose, and close interactions between multiple similar-looking individuals. This thesis addresses that challenge by adapting a memory-based visual tracking architecture into a real-time multi-person re-identification and tracking system for social robotics.

The proposed system extends the underlying segmentation and tracking pipeline with mechanisms aimed at improving identity consistency under ambiguity and interruption. Rather than treating tracking as a purely frame-to-frame problem, the approach emphasizes persistent identity reasoning across time, enabling the robot to recover the correct target after temporary disappearance and to remain stable in dynamic multi-person scenarios.

The resulting framework is designed for deployment in social-robot settings, where robustness, temporal continuity, and reliable target recovery are essential. The thesis evaluates the system qualitatively and quantitatively through challenging sequences involving occlusion, re-entry, and crowded interaction, showing how the proposed modifications improve identity-preserving tracking behavior compared with the baseline model.
