Robust Multi-Person Re-Identification and Tracking for Social Robotics

Inês Sousa
Thesis Code Demo

Why this matters

In social robotics, tracking a person is not only about following motion. A robot must preserve each person’s identity over time, even when people become occluded, leave the field of view, or reappear later in a different position or pose. This becomes especially difficult in multi-person environments where nearby individuals may look similar and interactions are dynamic.

Standard tracking pipelines often work well while the target remains continuously visible, but they can fail when identity must be recovered after ambiguity or disappearance. In a real robot, these failures can lead to identity switches, lost targets, and unstable behavior. This work focuses on making identity consistency more robust for long-term person-aware interaction.

Method overview

This thesis adapts a memory-based visual tracking architecture into a real-time multi-person re-identification and tracking system for social robotics. The objective is not only to segment and track people frame by frame, but also to preserve identity consistently across interruptions, occlusions, close interactions, and reappearance events.

The resulting system combines visual tracking, memory, and identity-aware reasoning to reduce identity switches and improve robustness in realistic robotic scenarios.

Normal tracking mode

When the target is confidently tracked, the system remains in normal tracking mode. In this mode, identity is maintained using recent visual evidence and temporal memory, enabling efficient and stable online tracking during continuous observation.

This mode is designed for smooth frame-to-frame operation while preserving a consistent representation of each tracked person.

Reacquisition mode

When confidence drops due to occlusion, disappearance, or ambiguity, the system switches to reacquisition mode. In this stage, candidate detections are evaluated to recover the correct identity before returning to normal tracking.

This is especially important in crowded scenes, after temporary exits from the field of view, or when multiple similar-looking people interact closely.
